1. Research Context and Questions
The EU AI Act (2024) mandates conformity assessments for high-risk AI systems - yet the audit profession has no standardised technical methodology for conducting them, and no automated tooling exists. Current AI audits are manual, bespoke, inconsistent, and expensive: a typical engagement runs 12-16 weeks and costs £50,000-£150,000. Meanwhile, organisations face a compliance deadline of August 2026. The gap between regulatory obligation and practical audit capability is one of the most pressing unsolved problems in applied AI governance.

This thesis addresses that gap by delivering two interlocking contributions: (1) UAGF-TAM - a Universal Technical AI Audit Methodology defining a 6-phase audit protocol with 18 standardised artefact templates, mapped to EU AI Act Articles 9, 43, and Annex III; and (2) AAA - the Autonomous AI Auditor (AAA), built using appropriate agentic and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) techniques, which executes the 6-phase UAGF-TAM protocol through a multi-agent pipeline to produce a full EU AI Act-compliant audit report. The system will be validated on three AI systems across three sectors.

Central Research Question: Can a multi-agent AI system reliably execute a 6-phase EU AI Act conformity audit protocol, producing reports of comparable completeness and regulatory coverage to human expert reviewers - in a fraction of the time and cost?

Sub-Questions
1. What 6-phase audit protocol and 18 artefact templates are necessary and sufficient to satisfy EU AI Act Articles 9, 43, and Annex III requirements for high-risk AI systems?
2. How does a python-constraint CSP model correctly route mandatory vs optional audit tests by EU AI Act risk tier (minimal/limited/high)?
3. What is the completeness score and regulatory coverage percentage of AAA audit reports compared to human expert reviewers across 3 case studies?
4. How does AAA performance generalise across AI system types: tabular classification (Finance), time-series forecasting (Retail), a live production system (Hamburg Hub), and an LLM-based or agentic system validated via the UAGF-TAM-L pathway?
5. What adaptation effort does AAA require for each new AI system type, and what does this imply for commercial scalability?
Theoretical Foundation and Cohort Context
The thesis draws on three bodies of work. AI audit methodology (Mokander et al. 2023; Koshiyama et al. 2022) establishes the practitioner gap: audits exist but are manual, inconsistent, and not mapped to specific regulatory requirements. Multi-agent LLM systems (Wang et al. 2024; multi-agent framework documentation) provide the technical architecture for decomposing a complex audit workflow into specialised, coordinated agents. EU AI Act conformity assessment requirements (Articles 9, 43, Annex III) provide the regulatory specification that the UAGF-TAM protocol must satisfy. Within the cohort, S5 sits at the apex of the UAGF product suite: S4 produces the 38-control governance score; S5 AAA calls S4 CGSA as an API endpoint for the governance phase of the audit. The JSON schema is agreed with the S4 student by Week 4; after that, development is fully independent.
2. Methodology
The study proceeds across three phases: protocol design, AAA system development, and four-case validation.
Phase 1 - UAGF-TAM Protocol Design (Weeks 1-4)
The 6-phase UAGF-TAM protocol is designed on paper before a single line of code is written. Each phase is specified with: objective, mandatory evidence items, optional evidence items, the corresponding artefact template, and the EU AI Act article it satisfies. The six phases are: (1) Scope - EU AI Act risk classification; (2) Data - data quality, completeness, and bias assessment; (3) Model - performance, explainability, and fairness; (4) Output - output pattern and fairness testing; (5) Ops - monitoring, logging, and incident response documentation review; (6) Report - full audit report compilation. A conditional UAGF-TAM-L branch is embedded in Phase 1: when the system is identified as LLM-based or agentic, Phases 2-4 are replaced by the LLM/Agentic pathway (golden dataset assessment, grounding/consistency/hallucination testing, and differential prompt testing). A python-constraint model encodes which phases are mandatory vs optional per risk tier, ensuring the AAA system routes correctly for minimal, limited, and high-risk systems. If S4 is in the same cohort, the 38-control JSON API schema is agreed at a joint meeting by Week 4.
Phase 2 - AAA System Development (Weeks 5-13)
The AAA is built incrementally. Weeks 5-7 establish a linear pipeline (Agents 1-3, no branching) tested end-to-end on the UCI German Credit dataset - the goal is a rough but complete report output before adding complexity. Weeks 8-9 add Agents 4 and 5 and the python-constraint routing logic. Weeks 10-11 add Agent 6 (Report) and reportlab PDF generation, producing a complete 2-hour audit report. Weeks 12-13 add conditional routing logic for risk-tier branching, tool calling for document parsing using appropriate RAG techniques, pytest unit tests per agent, and a GitHub Actions CI/CD pipeline. The system is tested on German Credit at each stage - never move to the next phase until the current one produces correct outputs.
Phase 3 - Four-Case Study Validation (Weeks 14-17)
Case Study 1 (Weeks 14-15) audits a Finance model on UCI German Credit: full AAA run, with supervisor review of completeness and regulatory coverage on this case study. Case Study 2 (Weeks 14-15, parallel) audits a Retail forecasting model on M5 data, adapting SHAP to TreeExplainer for time-series. Case Study 3 (Weeks 16-17) audits a live Hamburg Hub production AI system - the most valuable validation, requiring the data privacy agreement signed in Week 1. Findings are discussed with the company contact and sign-off obtained for report publication. Case Study 4 (Weeks 16-17) audits an LLM-based or agentic system sourced from Hamburg Hub or an open dataset, validating the UAGF-TAM-L pathway. All four cases are compiled into a comparison table.
